# Permission research: "Opus No 1"

**Goal:** Get permission to use the recording as the soundtrack for a
**non-commercial** light-art performance at Burning Man (free, art context, no
recording sold, no tickets sold for it).

**Bottom line:** This is one of the easiest rights situations you'll ever hit.
The famous Cisco hold music is **"Opus No. 1"**, written and recorded in 1989 by
**Tim Carleton** (with **Darrick Deel**). Tim **still owns 100% of the song** —
it was never assigned to Cisco; Cisco merely holds a **non-exclusive** license.
There is **no record label and no traditional publisher** in the middle: Tim
self-distributes (via DistroKid) and **personally handles all sync/licensing
requests** through a dedicated email he publishes himself. He and Deel
originally "put the music out there for free." For a free, non-commercial art
use, a direct one-paragraph email to Tim is almost certainly all you need.

## Detailed findings

### Master recording owner / record label
- **Owner: Tim Carleton himself (100%).** The 1989 4-track recording was never
  assigned to Cisco. In his own correction to the widely-repeated "never made a
  penny" story, Carleton stated: *"the ownership was never transferred to Cisco
  and it has since been used for sync in TV, Film and online streaming. Today,
  Cisco licenses the music from me and I still own 100% of the song."*
- **Label: none (self-released).** Modern releases ("Opus No. 1" single, and
  the 2014 "in stereo" version) are distributed by **DistroKid** — a
  self-distribution service, not a label. No indie label sits between Jeff and
  the artist; the artist is directly reachable. **Confidence: High.**

### Publisher / songwriter(s) / PRO
- **Songwriters:** Tim Carleton and Darrick Deel (some releases credit
  "Tim Carleton & Darrick Deel"; the Cisco-facing story credits Carleton as
  primary author with Deel recording/engineering). **Confidence: High.**
- **Publisher:** No third-party publisher discoverable; Carleton self-administers
  and fields sync directly (Opus1Sync@gmail.com). **Confidence: Med** (absence
  of a publisher is inferred from self-admin + DistroKid distribution; not a
  registered-catalog lookup).
- **PRO:** No PRO affiliation confirmed in public sources. Given self-admin and
  the "released for free" history, formal PRO registration may be minimal or
  absent. **Confidence: Low** (not verified either way — but irrelevant for a
  free non-commercial art use, where you need the owner's yes, not a PRO
  blanket license).

### Management / booking
- **None.** No manager, agent, or booking entity — this is a self-managed
  hobbyist artist. The sync email is effectively "management." **Confidence:
  High** (no representation surfaced anywhere).

### Direct artist contact paths
- **Sync/licensing email: Opus1Sync@gmail.com** — self-published for Ad/Sync
  inquiries. **Best channel. Confidence: High.**
- **YouTube: @timcarleton** — https://www.youtube.com/@timcarleton (where the
  sync email is listed; channel messaging/About as backup). **Confidence: Med.**
- **Spotify artist page:**
  https://open.spotify.com/artist/3ZHMNwUo3be6Kj59KRsPXS (identity confirmation,
  not a contact channel).
- **Bandcamp:** No confirmed official Tim Carleton Bandcamp artist page (a
  "GammaFlow" Bandcamp track of Opus No. 1 is a cover/upload, not him). So the
  usual "message the artist on Bandcamp" route does **not** apply here — the
  Gmail is the equivalent direct inbox. **Confidence: Med.**
- **Instagram/Twitter:** No verified official handle located. **Confidence:
  Low** — don't rely on DMs; the Gmail is the intended door.

## Why this is likely a "yes"
- One owner, who published a licensing inbox precisely to say yes to uses.
- The use is the flattering kind: free, non-commercial, art, generous credit,
  no recording sold. It adds to the song's cult legend rather than competing
  with any revenue.
- The artist has repeatedly, publicly embraced the song's meme status — low ego
  risk, high novelty appeal.
- The ~50/50 "no reply vs. yes" odds artists warned about mostly come from label
  /publisher/PRO gatekeepers who don't read art requests. Here you skip all of
  them and reach the decision-maker's own inbox — which should skew the odds
  well toward a reply.
