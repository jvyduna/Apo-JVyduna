# "Chrome Country" — Oneohtrix Point Never: Artist's Stated Vision

**Track:** "Chrome Country," the closing track (track 10, ~5:15) of *R Plus Seven*,
Oneohtrix Point Never's (Daniel Lopatin's) sixth album and his Warp Records debut,
released September 30, 2013. Artist confirmed: Daniel Lopatin, the Brooklyn
electronic musician — not to be confused with any namesake.

## Synthesis

Lopatin has said little that is *specifically* about "Chrome Country," but what
exists is pointed, and it sits inside a well-documented album-level vision. The
guiding idea of *R Plus Seven* is "musical objects": sounds treated as discrete,
placed things that decorate a virtual room — stable yet unreal, so that "when you
touch the wall maybe your hand goes through it." He leaned on the *uncanny valley*
of early digital gear (Korg M1/Wavestation ersatz-instrument presets) and on
text-to-speech voices that "attempt to have personality... but ultimately fail."
"Chrome Country" is where this culminates: gauzy, near-corny synthetic choir and
church-organ grandeur twisted toward something ambiguous, ending the record on a
luminous, artificial transcendence. The mood he attached to the whole record was
"domestic bliss" and calm, filtered through object-oriented-ontology reading.

## Findings (each sourced)

- On the voices in "Chrome Country": Lopatin says they are "almost corny" but
  "twisted in what sound like unpleasant directions"; he likes that such voices
  "attempt to have personality – they almost articulate something specific but
  ultimately fail," letting him "twist them around to intone something that I'd
  like to say in a non-direct, semiotic way." — The Quietus interview (via search
  extract; page returns 403 to direct fetch) https://thequietus.com/news/oneohtrix-point-never-r-plus-seven-interview/
- The synthetic voices came from text-to-speech: "One of them was a text-to-speech
  program that is fairly realistic, and I've actually recognised some of the voices
  in commercials – I heard one for a Virgin Airlines commercial." — The Quietus,
  same source. https://thequietus.com/news/oneohtrix-point-never-r-plus-seven-interview/
- Method for the album's voices generally: "We made all of these scripts and had
  text-to-speech programs read back all of these scripts... Then we sliced it up,
  put it chromatically on the keyboard with sampler instruments." — Electronic Beats
  https://www.electronicbeats.net/simulacra-simulation-an-interview-with-oneohtrix-point-never
- Aesthetic of artifice / uncanny valley: "That sort of creepy moment right before
  things are real is a sweet spot for me"; love of the "early ersatz, uncanny valley
  attempt at emulating real instruments" (Korg Wavestation/M1). — Electronic Beats
  https://www.electronicbeats.net/simulacra-simulation-an-interview-with-oneohtrix-point-never
- Core concept ("musical objects"): "I just want to create a room and decorate it
  with musical objects... those objects... naked and stable... but when you touch
  the wall maybe your hand goes through it"; sounds as "acute choices... that create
  a sense of place." Album framed as "calm" / "domestic bliss," influenced by
  object-oriented ontology. — Wikipedia (citing Stereogum/Quietus)
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_Plus_Seven
- Compositional stance: he worked "more intuitively, like the way a songwriter
  would work," minimizing repetition (saving hypnosis "for a live show"); calls the
  record "ornamental" with "flashes of light." — Stereogum Q&A
  https://stereogum.com/1459762/qa-daniel-lopatin-aka-oneohtrix-point-never-on-the-making-of-his-excellent-new-album-r-plus-seven/interviews
- Visual companion (not Lopatin's stated intent, but adjacent): the "Chrome Country"
  video repurposes footage from Ben Wheatley's 2013 film *A Field in England*. No
  dedicated director/artist statement for the video surfaced. — search corroboration,
  video https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FbqBXkmukr4 ; film
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Field_in_England

## Confidence

- **Solid:** The two "Chrome Country"-specific quotes (corny/twisted voices;
  text-to-speech + Virgin Airlines) and the album-level vision (musical objects,
  uncanny-valley artifice, TTS method, "domestic bliss"/OOO framing). Multiple
  interviews converge.
- **Thin / caveats:** The Quietus quotes come via search-engine extract because the
  live page 403s WebFetch; wording is likely near-verbatim but treat as
  paraphrase-adjacent until re-confirmed. No primary source frames "Chrome Country"
  as an intentional *closer* statement or explains its church-organ/choir imagery in
  the artist's own words — that reading is inferred. The video's meaning has no
  artist statement; the *A Field in England* footage link is community-reported, not
  from Warp/OPN.
