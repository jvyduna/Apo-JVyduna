# "Chrome Country" — Critic Impressions

Artist: **Oneohtrix Point Never** (Daniel Lopatin). Track: **"Chrome Country,"** the
5:15 closing track of **R Plus Seven** (Warp Records, 2013). Distinct from the
Australian dance act "Oneohtrix" namesakes — this is Lopatin's Warp debut. There is
a music video for the track directed by Jon Rafman and Nate Boyce.

## Synthesis

Critics treat "Chrome Country" as the album's serene, quasi-religious benediction —
a comparatively linear, hymn-like closer that swaps R Plus Seven's restless MIDI
churn for a slow build toward massed church organ and choir. The dominant register
is warmth, awe, and digital-sublime "holy music," with recurring reach for
neoclassical/minimalist reference points (Philip Glass, Steve Reich, Art of Noise)
rather than Lopatin's usual synth-drone lineage. Retrospective writing rates it among
his finest single tracks.

## Findings

- **Resident Advisor** frames "Chrome Country" as the "serene closing track," "warm,
  dizzying and flat-out blissful," and one of two "uncharacteristically linear songs
  that bookend the album," relying on "massive church organs and choirs to reach
  their climaxes, as if ushering listeners in and out of a holy place." RA also likens
  it to "one of Art of Noise's many deep 'Moments in Love' remixes … stitched
  together." — https://ra.co/reviews/13948

- **Stereogum (R Plus Seven Turns 10)** calls it "one of the greatest electronic
  tracks ever made," saying it "marries overwhelming digitalism and human emotion in a
  captivating, singular way." Describes the structure: from "a cloud of analog chords"
  through robotic vocal and piano motifs that "grow sharper and more dissonant," into
  "a droning bed of strings and church organ"; the terrain is "unpredictable,
  borderline-neoclassical … more similar to the sound of Philip Glass than Tangerine
  Dream." —
  https://stereogum.com/2237213/oneohtrix-point-never-r-plus-seven-turns-10/reviews/the-anniversary

- **Tiny Mix Tapes** discusses "Chrome Country" (with "Boring Angel") as evidence of
  the album's "religious dimensions"; R Plus Seven topped TMT's 2013 year-end list. —
  https://www.tinymixtapes.com/music-review/oneohtrix-point-never-r-plus-seven

- **Pitchfork (Mark Richardson, Best New Music, 8.4)** — album-level: "you don't
  listen to this record thinking about theory; it's beautiful stuff, with chords and
  tunes and sections you remember"; the work is "strange and otherworldly and, most
  importantly, rich with feeling." — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_Plus_Seven
  (Pitchfork original: pitchfork.com/reviews/albums/18555 — could not fetch directly)

- **Rolling Stone (Andy Battaglia)** — album described as "holy music, even if wholly
  weird," compared to Philip Glass and Steve Reich for rhythm and repetition.
  **Filter (Marty Sartini Garner)** — R Plus Seven is "the dissection of a heart."
  **The Skinny (Bram E. Gieben)** — "each track contains distinct movements," inviting
  classical-composition comparison. — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_Plus_Seven

## Confidence

- **Solid:** RA and Stereogum quotes are track-specific and fetched directly. The
  "serene closer / church organ + choir / holy music" consensus and the Glass–Reich–
  Art of Noise reference cluster are well corroborated.
- **Thinner:** Pitchfork/Rolling Stone/Filter/Skinny quotes are album-level and reached
  via Wikipedia aggregation, not the original pages (Pitchfork blocked direct fetch).
  Music-video director attribution (Rafman/Boyce) is from search context, not a fetched
  primary source. No Quietus or PopMatters track-specific review was located.
