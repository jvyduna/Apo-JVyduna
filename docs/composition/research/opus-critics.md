# Opus No 1 — Critic Impressions

## Synthesis

There is **no traditional professional-critic corpus** for this track: it was
never released as an album single or reviewed by Pitchfork, The Quietus,
Resident Advisor, Tiny Mix Tapes, AllMusic, or PopMatters. "Opus No. 1" is a
1989 garage recording that became Cisco's default IP-phone hold music and, via
that ubiquity, a viral curiosity. The nearest thing to critical writing lives in
**cultural-journalism and music-blog coverage** (Stereogum, This American Life,
The Atlantic, Mental Floss) and treats it less as a composition to evaluate than
as a phenomenon — an accidental earworm whose emotional charge comes from
half-remembered familiarity. The recurring critical vocabulary is "soothing,"
"smooth jazz," and "generic '80s synth." Its one genuine art-world validation is
Tom Krell (How To Dress Well) sampling it on "Precious Love."

## Findings

- **No formal reviews exist.** Targeted searches for Pitchfork/Quietus/RA/etc.
  coverage returned only forum threads, IT-industry blogs, and general-interest
  explainers — no music-crit reviews of the piece or any "album."
  https://www.talkclassical.com/threads/opus-no-1-or-the-cisco-call-manager-hold-music.80306/
- **Stereogum (closest to a critic voice):** frames the tune as "generic '80s
  synth" that Krell "repurposed beautifully" into "one of the most memorable
  tracks from a very memorable album," and captures the uncanny-familiarity hook
  — "something in the back of your head is gnawing away at you: Where have I heard
  this? Why is it vaguely annoying?" Headline calls it "soothing."
  https://stereogum.com/1687898/the-story-behind-the-soothing-cisco-hold-music-sampled-on-how-to-dress-wells-precious-love/news
- **This American Life (ep. 553/516):** casts it as "one of the most widely-heard
  earworms that no one ever intended to listen to," and surfaces polarized
  listener reception — "Best hold music ever made," "So addictively pleasant.
  Please, put me back on hold so I can listen to it some more."
  https://www.thisamericanlife.org/553/transcript
- **Genre framing:** described as "a 30 second uptempo smooth jazz cut that loops"
  and grouped with smooth jazz / light-classical / bossa "beautiful music"
  hold-music tradition. https://www.onholdmessagingdirect.com/blog/the-most-overused-piece-of-on-hold-music-in-history/
- **Origin (well documented):** written 1989 by Tim Carleton (then 16, a
  "Yanni-loving computer nerd" with a drum machine + synth in his parents'
  California garage) and Darrick Deel on a 4-track; Deel later brought it to Cisco.
  https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/68163/ciscos-default-hold-music-was-written-16-year-old-computer-nerd
- **Licensing / who to ask:** Carleton and Deel were paid only for the original
  work and got nothing from the phone rollout ("Not a penny"), but ownership was
  **never transferred to Cisco** — Carleton owns 100% and now licenses it for
  sync (TV, film, streaming), including a 2023 Anheuser-Busch Super Bowl spot. For
  a non-commercial art use, **Tim Carleton is the rights holder to approach.**
  https://www.replicant.com/blog/the-surprising-roots-of-one-of-the-most-common-hold-music-songs

## Confidence

- **Solid:** the absence of mainstream music-crit reviews; the composition/origin
  story; that Carleton owns the copyright and is the person to ask; the Stereogum
  and This American Life characterizations (direct quotes).
- **Thin:** exact-length/genre claims ("30-second smooth jazz loop") come from a
  vendor blog, not a critic; there is no deep structural/emotional analysis to
  cite because no critic wrote one. Distinguish this Tim Carleton (composer) from
  same-name namesakes; the credited duo is Tim Carleton & Darrick Deel.
