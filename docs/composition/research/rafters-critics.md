# "Rafters" by Lusine — Professional Critic Impressions

## Synthesis

**Correction to the context seed:** "Rafters" is *not* on *Sensorimotor* (2017). It is
the penultimate track on Lusine's ninth album **_Long Light_** (Ghostly International,
GI-427, released **September 15, 2023**). Lusine is Jeff McIlwain, a Seattle-based
electronic producer, and 2023 marked twenty years on Ghostly — the seed's artist and
label are correct; the album is wrong.

"Rafters" is a short (~2:58) **instrumental**, grouped by press and critics with
"Faceless" and "Plateau" as the album's **ambient-minded, contemplative core** — the
introspective counterweight to the record's brighter microhouse/minimal-techno
head-nodders. Critical reception of the album was positive-to-moderate ("generally
favorable"); there is **no Pitchfork, Resident Advisor, or Quietus review** of this
album that I could locate — the outlet coverage is smaller (HearFeel, HHV, Boomkat,
AllMusic, Igloo). Track-specific critical writing on "Rafters" itself is thin; the
strongest single phrase is HearFeel's.

## Findings

- **HearFeel (Nov 7, 2023)** — the only source with a direct, evocative line on the
  track: names "the penultimate 'Rafters' in its **fluted urgency**" as part of the
  album's "**nervous and self-conscious core, fearful of stagnation**," alongside
  "Faceless" and "Plateau." Frames the album as "a record of **clarity and
  refinement** from someone who has a command of their craft," with "the pace… hot and
  fast, unwilling to languish," introspective moments "sprinkled judiciously" and
  "set within bright, even jovial, Microhouse and Minimal Techno structures."
  https://hearfeel.co.uk/2023/11/07/lusine-long-light/

- **Ghostly / Bandcamp press blurb** — positions "Rafters" (2:58, instrumental) with
  "Faceless" and "Plateau" as "ambient-minded" pieces and "Lusine's strongest
  instrumental expressions," contrasting the vocal-pop tracks. Album described as
  "the most direct in his catalog," "laser-focused, process-driven," "structurally
  straightforward, tight, and bright," built on "signature looping patterns and
  textures… dynamic yet minimalist."
  https://lusine.bandcamp.com/album/long-light

- **HHV Magazine — Björn Bischoff (Oct 25, 2023)** — album "oscillates between IDM,
  ambient and pop structures" with rhythm as its driving force; "a charming piece of
  music that courts and entices, but never forces." (Does not name "Rafters"; focuses
  on the Sarah Jaffe vocal cut "Zero to Sixty.")
  https://www.hhv-mag.com/review/lusine-long-light/?lang=en

- **Inverted Audio** — album interweaves Lusine's "trademark sound" with "pop-infused
  vocals"; quotes McIlwain on the title concept: "an age of high anxiety… a fun house
  mirror situation." No individual note on "Rafters."
  https://inverted-audio.com/lusine-lines-long-light-ghostly-international/

- **AllMusic** — hosts a *Long Light* review page (star rating exists) but the page
  returned HTTP 403 to automated fetch; content unverified here.
  https://www.allmusic.com/album/long-light-mw0004047873

- **Album of the Year** — aggregate critic score ~70; notably **few** professional
  reviews aggregated (majors like Pitchfork/RA absent), confirming limited critic
  coverage. https://www.albumoftheyear.org/album/727821-lusine-long-light.php

- **Artist identity / namesake note** — "Lusine" here is Jeff McIlwain (also recorded
  as Lusine ICL); distinct from the common Armenian given name "Lusine" and unrelated
  vocalists. Confirmed across Ghostly, Boomkat, RA artist profile.
  https://ra.co/dj/lusine

## Confidence

- **Solid:** Track is on *Long Light* (Ghostly, 2023), not *Sensorimotor*; "Rafters"
  is a ~3-min instrumental read by critics/press as ambient/contemplative, paired with
  "Faceless" and "Plateau"; HearFeel's "fluted urgency" / "nervous… fearful of
  stagnation" line; album's overall bright-microhouse-with-introspective-pockets shape.
- **Thin:** Almost no *track-level* critic prose beyond HearFeel — "Rafters" is
  minor within the album's coverage. No tier-1 outlet (Pitchfork/RA/Quietus/TMT)
  reviewed the album; AllMusic text unretrieved. Emotional analysis of "Rafters"
  specifically is largely inferred from its grouping, not directly quoted.
